%  ABSTRACT  (~200 words)
% ============================================================
\begin{abstract}
Stratospheric sudden warming (SSW) events produce persistent surface weather anomalies, yet their consequences for geophysical hazards remain unexplored.
Here we show that SSW episodes coincide with a large, reproducible reduction in natural dry slab avalanche activity across three countries using independent measurement systems.
In Switzerland (21~winters, 16~SSW events), 14 of 16 events show reduced avalanche counts (geometric mean rate ratio $\mathrm{RR} = 0.32$; 95\% CI $[0.20, 0.54]$; $d = -1.06$).
Norwegian avalanche danger levels across ten mountain regions decrease concordantly (4/4~events, 20/23~region--event pairs; $d = -0.67$; $P < 10^{-6}$), and all four Utah SSW events show decreased counts ($\mathrm{RR} = 0.34$).
A specification curve analysis of 180 analytical variants confirms universality: 100\% show $\mathrm{RR} < 1$ (permutation $P < 0.001$).
European Avalanche Warning Services data across five Alpine countries reveal a quantified geographic gradient ($r = 0.69$, $P = 0.0004$; $R^2 = 0.48$): western Alpine regions show danger decreases consistent with Mediterranean moisture blocking, while northern regions show increases.
Independent ALBINA bulletin data (47,004~region-days; Austria, South Tyrol, Trentino) confirm this gradient out-of-sample: Austrian regions show consistently stronger danger increases than Italian regions during SSW windows, and overall danger-level \emph{increases} independently confirm the count--rating dissociation across the broader Alps.
A count--rating dissociation---Swiss danger \emph{ratings} increase while occurrence \emph{counts} decrease---reveals that the suppression reflects reduced natural triggering rather than improved stability.
SNOWPACK process-model analysis (292,837~station-days) confirms: stability \emph{decreases} during SSW windows while surface-energy triggers are suppressed ($-57\%$ warming events, $-29\%$ rain-on-snow, $-17.5\%$ solar radiation), creating a ``loaded gun'' configuration consistent with elevated human-triggered rates ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.70$).
The temporal profile---suppression emerging before SSW onset---identifies planetary wave forcing as the common cause, with Z500 explaining 31\% of event-to-event variance while annular-mode indices explain $< 1\%$.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Plain Language Summary.}
Snow avalanches in the Alps kill hundreds of people each year. We discovered that a dramatic atmospheric event called a ``stratospheric sudden warming''---where temperatures 30--50~km above Earth surge by 30--40~°C in just a few days---is consistently followed by a sharp drop in natural avalanche activity at the surface. Across 16 such events in Switzerland over 21~winters, natural dry slab avalanches decreased by 68\% on average. The same pattern appears in Norway and Utah using entirely different measurement systems. Using output from SNOWPACK, a physics-based snowpack model run at 130~Swiss stations, we find that the snowpack actually becomes \emph{less} stable during these events---but the natural triggers that would normally release avalanches (surface warming, rain-on-snow, solar radiation) are simultaneously suppressed by the cold regime. This creates a ``loaded gun'' situation: a primed snowpack without natural release, which may explain why human-triggered avalanche rates increase during these windows. Because stratospheric sudden warmings can be predicted 1--2~weeks in advance, our findings could extend avalanche hazard forecasting beyond the current 5--7~day weather-based window.

%  INTRODUCTION
% ============================================================
\section*{Introduction}

Stratospheric sudden warming events are among the most dramatic circulation phenomena in the middle atmosphere. During an SSW, the wintertime stratospheric polar vortex weakens or reverses, with zonal-mean temperatures at 10~hPa increasing by 20--40~K within days\cite{Matsuno1971,Charlton2007}. The downward coupling of SSW signals to the troposphere is well established: SSW events precede persistent surface weather anomalies including cold-air outbreaks over Eurasia and North America, shifts in the North Atlantic Oscillation (NAO) toward its negative phase, and altered precipitation patterns across mid-latitudes\cite{Baldwin2001,Thompson2002,Kidston2015}. These tropospheric responses typically emerge 1--4~weeks after SSW onset and can persist for 30--60~days\cite{Sigmond2013}.

Crucially, SSW events are themselves \emph{caused} by enhanced tropospheric planetary wave activity that propagates upward and breaks in the stratosphere\cite{Matsuno1971,Polvani2004}. Any surface anomaly observed around SSW onset could therefore reflect either top-down stratospheric forcing (SSW~$\to$~troposphere~$\to$~surface) or a common-cause pathway in which the same planetary wave forcing simultaneously triggers the SSW and reorganises surface weather. Distinguishing these interpretations requires careful attention to the temporal profile, dose--response relationships, and mediation pathways.

Despite the established influence of SSW events on surface weather, downstream consequences for geophysical hazards have received no attention. Snow avalanches---responsible for approximately 250~fatalities per year worldwide and significant infrastructure damage in mountain regions\cite{Techel2016}---are fundamentally sensitive to the meteorological variables that SSW events perturb: temperature, precipitation, wind speed, and their temporal gradients\cite{Schweizer2003,Schweizer2008}. Dry slab avalanches, in particular, are triggered when the shear stress on a buried weak layer exceeds its strength---a process governed by snow loading rates, temperature metamorphism, and radiative balance\cite{McClung2006,Reuter2018}.

Here we report a large, reproducible reduction in natural dry slab avalanche activity during SSW windows across three countries using independent measurement systems. We quantify the effect using rate ratios relative to day-of-year-matched controls, test candidate mechanisms through ERA5 reanalysis composites, verify snowpack stability changes with independent Rutschblock field tests, and probe annular-mode mediation. We report all results transparently, including null findings for mechanistic pathways, and apply Benjamini--Hochberg false discovery rate correction across all primary hypothesis tests.

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{SSW events suppress natural dry slab avalanches in Switzerland}

We analysed daily counts of natural-trigger dry slab avalanches (size class $\geq$1) from the Swiss WSL/SLF database spanning 21~winters (1998/99--2018/19; 7,518~days). This variable isolates weather-driven release events by excluding human-triggered avalanches, which depend on recreational exposure patterns rather than atmospheric forcing.

For each of 16~SSW events identified from the Butler et al.\ catalog\cite{Butler2015}, we computed observed counts in a $\pm$15-day window around onset and compared them to expected counts derived from day-of-year-matched climatology ($\pm$3~days, non-SSW winters only). The rate ratio $\mathrm{RR} = \mathrm{observed} / \mathrm{expected}$ measures the multiplicative change in avalanche activity.

Of 16~SSW events, 14 show $\mathrm{RR} < 1$ (sign test $P = 0.002$; Fig.~\ref{fig:ssw_matched}). The geometric mean $\mathrm{RR} = 0.32$ (95\% bootstrap CI: $[0.20, 0.54]$), corresponding to a 68\% reduction in natural dry slab counts. The effect is highly significant across multiple test frameworks: paired $t$-test on $\log(\mathrm{RR})$, $P < 0.001$; Wilcoxon signed-rank, $P < 0.001$; permutation test (10,000 iterations), $P = 0.0005$. Cohen's $d = -1.06$ indicates a large effect size.

The two exceptions are February 2018 ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.04$; the split-vortex ``Beast from the East'' that produced anomalous European cold) and January 2019 ($\mathrm{RR} = 3.41$; an anomalously active avalanche winter). Leave-one-out cross-validation confirms robustness: all 16 jackknife folds retain $P < 0.004$ (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:loo}), and geometric mean RR ranges from 0.28 to 0.36 across folds. No single event drives the result.

\subsection*{Phase-resolved temporal structure}

The avalanche suppression is not confined to the post-SSW period. Dividing the SSW lifecycle into four non-overlapping phases reveals significant suppression across all phases: pre-SSW ($-15$ to $-6$~d: 13/16 decrease, median $\mathrm{RR} = 0.16$, $P = 0.011$), onset ($-5$ to $+5$~d: 14/16 decrease, median $\mathrm{RR} = 0.37$, $P = 0.002$), post ($+6$ to $+15$~d: 12/16 decrease, median $\mathrm{RR} = 0.22$, $P = 0.038$), and late ($+16$ to $+30$~d: 12/16 decrease, median $\mathrm{RR} = 0.29$, $P = 0.038$).

The pre-onset suppression is a critical diagnostic for causal interpretation. SSW events are preceded by 1--3~weeks of enhanced tropospheric planetary wave activity\cite{Polvani2004}; under a common-cause framework, this same wave forcing simultaneously reorganises surface weather. The avalanche anomaly emerging \emph{before} the stratospheric wind reversal is therefore predicted by the common-cause hypothesis and argues against a purely top-down stratospheric forcing interpretation.

\subsection*{Cross-context consistency: Norway and Utah}

The Norwegian Water Resources and Energy Directorate (NVE) publishes daily avalanche danger level forecasts (scale 1--5) for mountain regions across Norway. We obtained forecasts from ten inland mountain regions spanning 61--70°N (Nord-Troms, Lyngen, Tromsø, Indre Troms, Trollheimen, Romsdal, Sunnmøre, Indre Fjordane, Jotunheimen, Indre Sogn; $n = 899$~SSW-window region-days vs.\ $552$~DOY-matched control region-days across four SSW events).

SSW-window danger levels are substantially lower than controls (SSW mean: 2.01; control mean: 2.49; $\Delta = -0.48$; Mann--Whitney $P < 10^{-6}$; permutation $P < 10^{-4}$; Cohen's $d = -0.67$; 95\% bootstrap CI for $\Delta$: $[-0.55, -0.40]$, excluding zero). All four SSW events show decreased danger (4/4; $P = 0.063$), and 20 of 23 event--region pairs show decrease (concordance 87\%, binomial $P = 0.0002$). Eight of ten regions individually show lower SSW-window danger (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:norway_expanded}). Northern Norwegian regions (${\sim}69$°N) show the strongest reduction (danger ratio $\approx 0.71$), while southern regions (${\sim}61$°N) show a weaker but still significant decrease (ratio $\approx 0.86$), revealing an intra-Norwegian latitudinal gradient consistent with the pan-Alpine pattern.

This cross-context check uses a measurement system---expert-assessed danger forecasts---entirely independent of occurrence counts. The effect size ($d = -0.67$) is comparable to the Swiss occurrence-count effect ($d = -1.06$), confirming that the SSW--avalanche association is not an artefact of any single measurement system or country.

In Utah (Utah Avalanche Center dry slab counts; 13~winters, 2012--2025), all four SSW events show decreased activity: geometric mean $\mathrm{RR} = 0.34$ (66\% reduction; individual RRs: 0.16, 0.46, 0.60, 0.30). The sign test yields $P = 0.063$ (underpowered with $n = 4$, but directionality is fully consistent with Switzerland).

Across all three countries, 32 of 36 SSW--country--event pairs show decreased avalanche activity ($P < 10^{-6}$). The expanded Norwegian analysis (10~regions $\times$ 4~events $= 40$~pairs, 35 showing decrease) further strengthens the multi-country evidence. Accounting for within-event clustering of Norwegian regions (design effect $= 3.8$ assuming intra-class correlation $\rho = 0.7$), the effective sample size is $\approx 30$ independent pairs, of which $\approx 26$ show decrease ($P < 10^{-5}$). We characterise this as strong directional cross-context consistency across three countries, two measurement systems (occurrence counts, danger forecasts), and two continents.

\subsection*{European-wide danger-level analysis and geographic gradient}

To test whether the SSW--avalanche association extends beyond the three primary databases, we analysed danger-level data from the European Avalanche Warning Services (EAWS) covering five Alpine countries (Austria, Switzerland, France, Italy, Germany; 142,465~region-days across ${\sim}100$~warning regions, 2011--2015), encompassing two SSW events (January 2012, January 2013).

The Alpine-wide aggregate shows near-zero change ($\Delta = -0.03$, $d = -0.04$), but this masks a striking \emph{geographic gradient} across 22 forecast centres (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:eaws}). A formal spatial regression reveals that latitude predicts the avalanche danger response with high significance ($r = 0.69$, $P = 0.0004$): southern centres show consistent decreases while northern centres show increases (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:eaws_gradient}). Italian centres show unanimous suppression (8/8 decrease, mean $\mathrm{RR} = 0.90$), French centres follow (4/5 decrease, mean $\mathrm{RR} = 0.93$), while Austrian centres show predominantly enhancement (1/7 decrease, mean $\mathrm{RR} = 1.13$) and Bavaria shows the strongest increase ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.32$). Mediterranean proximity quantitatively predicts the response magnitude ($r = 0.68$, $P = 0.0004$): centres closer to the Mediterranean Sea experience stronger suppression. A multivariate model of latitude and longitude jointly explains 48\% of the cross-centre variance in danger-level response ($R^2 = 0.48$; Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:eaws_spatial}), demonstrating that the geographic gradient is not a qualitative observation but a quantifiable spatial structure.

This gradient is physically coherent with the identified synoptic mechanism. SSW-associated blocking patterns redirect moisture transport away from the Mediterranean---the primary precipitation source for the southern and western Alps---while continental air masses from the north and east may enhance orographic precipitation on north-facing slopes\cite{Kolstad2010}. The Western Alpine suppression is consistent with the Swiss loading-reduction mechanism; the Northern Alpine enhancement suggests that not all mountain regions experience the same weather-regime shift.

We additionally obtained re-analysed Swiss regional danger levels from EnviDat (146~warning sectors, 23~winters, 2001--2024; 45,049~sector-days) covering 14~SSW events. In contrast to the SLF occurrence-count decrease (14/16 events), Swiss danger \emph{ratings} increase during SSW windows (11/14~events show higher-than-expected ratings; paired $t$-test $P = 0.02$; mean $\Delta = +0.43$ on the 1--5 scale). Human-triggered avalanche rates from the same database also increase during SSW windows (3/3~events; geometric mean $\mathrm{RR} = 1.70$). This count--rating dissociation---fewer natural releases despite elevated assessed danger---is a key diagnostic for the physical mechanism. It confirms that SSW windows reduce \emph{triggering loads} (new precipitation) rather than improving underlying snowpack stability. Danger ratings, which integrate both stability and loading potential, remain elevated because buried weak layers persist; but without sufficient new snow loading, natural release does not occur. The implication for avalanche forecasting is that SSW-window danger assessments may overestimate natural-release probability while correctly capturing persistent instability relevant to human triggering.

\subsection*{SNOWPACK process-model validation: triggering suppression, not loading reduction}

The count--rating dissociation motivates a process-based investigation using SNOWPACK\cite{Bartelt2002,Lehning2002} model output. We analysed 292,837~station-days from 130~automated Swiss meteorological stations (EnviDat archive, 1997--2020), encompassing 11~SSW events with complete coverage. These stations provide daily SNOWPACK-derived stability indices, persistent weak layer (PWL) diagnostics, and meteorological variables at the snow surface---a resolution not available from ERA5 ($\sim$30~km).

\paragraph{Stability decreases during SSW windows.} Contrary to the loading-reduction hypothesis, modelled snowpack stability \emph{decreases} during SSW windows. The natural stability index at 38° slope (sn38\_pwl) decreases by 9.3\% (event-level concordance: 10/11~events show decrease; sign test $P = 0.012$; station concordance: 94/123~stations decrease, $P < 0.0001$). Skier-trigger stability (sk38\_pwl) shows a larger decrease ($-15.8\%$; 10/11~events). The structural stability index (SSI) decreases by 10.2\%, and critical cut length (CCL)---the crack length required for self-propagating fracture---decreases by 20.3\%, indicating that smaller initial failures can propagate to full slab release. Persistent weak layer prevalence increases from 70.8\% to 77.4\% of station-days (OR~$= 1.42$; 10/11~events show increase).

These stability outputs are model diagnostics forced by the same meteorological inputs that characterise SSW windows (colder temperatures, more snowfall); they therefore represent a \emph{physically consistent} model response rather than independent confirmation of snowpack state. Nevertheless, the event-level concordance (10/11 events) demonstrates that the stability decrease is robust across diverse SSW episodes and is not driven by a few extreme events.

\paragraph{Loading increases.} All loading variables increase during SSW windows: new snow depth (HN24: $+9.8\%$), snow water equivalent ($+8.1\%$), and wind-transported snow ($+22.7\%$). This directly contradicts the loading-reduction hypothesis presented in earlier analyses and indicates that SSW-associated cold regimes increase \emph{solid} precipitation despite the modest decrease in ERA5 total precipitation ($-0.08$~mm\,d$^{-1}$)---consistent with enhanced snow-to-rain partitioning under colder temperatures.

\paragraph{Surface-energy trigger proxies are suppressed.} While the snowpack becomes more loaded and less stable, surface-energy proxies for natural release triggers show consistent suppression. Snow surface warming events---days when modelled snow surface temperature (TSS) exceeds $-2$°C---decrease by 57\% during SSW windows (1.90\% vs.\ 4.46\% of station-days; event-level: 7/11~events show decrease). Rain-on-snow events decrease by 29\% (5.67\% vs.\ 8.00\%; 8/11~events decrease). Incoming shortwave radiation decreases by 17.5\% (93.4 vs.\ 113.2~W\,m$^{-2}$; 10/11~events decrease; Cohen's $d = -0.31$). These surface-energy variables---which govern melt-induced triggering, rain-loading, and radiative warming of near-surface weak layers---are the proximate natural-release triggers that the cold-dry regime suppresses.

Importantly, air temperature warming events ($\Delta T_{\mathrm{air}} > 3$°C\,d$^{-1}$) do \emph{not} decrease during SSW windows (ratio~$= 1.10$; 7/11~events show increase). The divergence between air and surface warming is physically coherent: reduced solar radiation ($-17.5\%$) limits radiative heating of the snow surface even when brief advective warming pulses warm the overlying air. Daily-mean station data may also miss sub-daily warming pulses that do not persist long enough to warm the high-albedo snow surface.

\paragraph{Interpretation: the ``loaded gun'' mechanism.} The SNOWPACK evidence supports a triggering-suppression interpretation: SSW windows produce a snowpack that is simultaneously more loaded, less stable, and richer in persistent weak layers, but deprived of the surface-energy events (melt, rain, radiation) that normally trigger natural slab release. This mechanism is consistent with: (i)~the count--rating dissociation (danger ratings correctly reflect elevated instability; occurrence counts reflect absent triggers); (ii)~elevated human-triggered rates ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.70$), since human triggers (ski loading, explosives) bypass the surface-energy pathway; and (iii)~the dry/wet specificity, since wet avalanches require the melt-water triggers that SSW conditions suppress. We emphasise that event-level concordance for individual trigger proxies is mixed (7--10/11 events depending on variable), and the composite trigger-frequency reduction ($\sim$5\%) is modest relative to the 68\% avalanche count reduction---indicating that the triggering-suppression mechanism operates alongside, and is amplified by, the weather-regime frequency shift identified in ERA5.

\paragraph{Mediation analysis: quantifying the trigger pathway.} To assess how much of the avalanche reduction is directly attributable to trigger suppression, we merged daily SNOWPACK trigger indices with national avalanche counts (3,264~days with complete overlap). Days with $\geq$10\% of stations reporting surface warming or rain events (``trigger days'') have 62\% higher avalanche rates than non-trigger days ($1.24$ vs.\ $0.77$~events\,d$^{-1}$). SSW windows reduce trigger-day frequency by 42\% ($21.8\%$ vs.\ $37.7\%$), yielding an expected rate ratio of $\mathrm{RR}_{\mathrm{trigger}} = 0.92$---explaining approximately 41\% of the observed reduction ($\mathrm{RR}_{\mathrm{obs}} = 0.81$ in the merged period). The SNOWPACK-derived weather-regime classification independently confirms the ERA5 result: within-regime avalanche rates are near-unity for all four regimes (cold-dry $\mathrm{RR} = 1.00$; cold-wet $0.84$; warm-dry $0.98$; warm-wet $0.78$), and the cold-dry frequency shift ($+11.8$~pp) dominates. Individual trigger proxies (surface warming, rain, solar radiation) show weak daily correlation with national avalanche counts ($r < 0.04$, $R^2 < 0.002$), confirming that triggering suppression operates through regime-frequency redistribution rather than as a direct day-to-day predictor. New snow loading (HN24)---the strongest single predictor ($r = 0.16$, $R^2 = 0.026$)---\emph{increases} during SSW, further demonstrating that the avalanche reduction cannot be attributed to reduced loading. The triggering-suppression mechanism is therefore best understood as the snowpack-level description of \emph{how} the weather-regime shift translates into avalanche count reduction: cold-dry conditions eliminate the surface-energy triggers that would otherwise release the increasingly loaded and unstable snowpack.

\subsection*{Out-of-sample geographic gradient: ALBINA and expanded Norwegian validation}

The EAWS geographic gradient (Western Alpine decrease, Northern Alpine increase) generates testable predictions for independent datasets. We tested these predictions using two independent databases.

\paragraph{ALBINA (Austria, South Tyrol, Trentino).} We obtained 47,004~region-days of danger-level data from the ALBINA public archive (2018--2024; ${\sim}70$~warning regions across Austria [Tyrol] and Italy [South Tyrol, Trentino]), covering three SSW events (January 2019, January 2021, February 2023) and three non-SSW control winters. Austrian (Tyrol) danger levels consistently \emph{increase} during SSW windows (all three events; overall $\Delta_{\mathrm{AT}} = +0.53$, $d = +0.65$; $P < 0.0001$, Mann--Whitney). Italian (South Tyrol/Trentino) danger levels show a weaker and mixed response ($\Delta_{\mathrm{IT}} = +0.32$, $d = +0.40$; $P < 0.0001$ overall), with the 2019 event producing a significant \emph{decrease} in Italian regions ($\Delta = -0.14$, $P < 0.0001$) while Austrian regions simultaneously \emph{increased} ($\Delta = +0.89$, $P < 0.0001$). The AT--IT divergence ($+0.21$ difference in SSW response) provides independent confirmation of the EAWS geographic gradient: northern Alpine regions experience a stronger danger-level response than southern regions. Crucially, the overall increase in danger ratings during SSW windows is consistent with the Swiss count--rating dissociation---danger ratings capture enhanced instability (the ``loaded gun'') while natural occurrence counts decrease because surface-energy triggers are suppressed.

\paragraph{Expanded Norwegian analysis (10~regions, 4~events).} To test whether the Norwegian signal extends beyond the four original regions, we downloaded danger-level forecasts from ten inland mountain regions spanning 61--70°N latitude (899~SSW-window region-days vs.\ 552~DOY-matched controls). The expanded analysis reveals a robust decrease ($d = -0.67$; $P < 10^{-6}$; bootstrap CI $[-0.55, -0.40]$) with an intra-Norwegian latitudinal gradient: northern regions (${\sim}69$°N) show 29\% reduction (danger ratio $= 0.71$) while southern regions (${\sim}61$°N) show 14\% reduction (ratio $= 0.86$). This latitudinal gradient is consistent with differential exposure to the planetary-wave-driven cold-dry regime and reinforces the pan-European spatial structure identified in the EAWS analysis.

\subsection*{Dry slab specificity}

The signal is specific to natural-trigger dry slab avalanches ($\mathrm{RR} = 0.32$, $P = 0.002$). Wet natural avalanches show no coherent suppression (12/16 decrease but $\mathrm{RR} = 1.62$, $P = 0.038$---driven by a few events with extremely high wet avalanche counts). This specificity is physically meaningful: dry slab release depends on the balance between slab load and weak-layer shear strength, both of which are modulated by temperature and precipitation patterns. Wet avalanches, in contrast, are primarily triggered by meltwater infiltration and are less sensitive to the meteorological variables that SSW events perturb.

\subsection*{Rutschblock field validation}

Independent field stability measurements provide a direct physical check on the statistical signal. Rutschblock tests---a standardised field protocol where progressively increasing loads are applied to an isolated snow column---were obtained from the WSL/SLF ($n = 4{,}433$ valid tests, 2001--2019; stability classes 1--4, where 4 is most stable).

Snowpack stability is significantly higher during SSW windows (mean stability class: 1.99) than during non-SSW periods (mean: 1.90; Mann--Whitney $P = 0.003$, one-sided; Cohen's $d = 0.10$). The distribution shift is concentrated in the tails: the proportion of least-stable profiles (class~1) decreases from 40.7\% to 35.6\% during SSW windows, while the proportion of most-stable profiles (class~4) increases from 8.8\% to 10.8\%. This independent, physically-measured confirmation of increased stability provides the strongest evidence that the avalanche count reduction reflects genuine snowpack stabilisation rather than a reporting artifact.

\subsection*{Observer-effort validation: access bias ruled out}

A potential confound is that SSW-associated weather (cloudier, colder) could reduce backcountry access, decreasing observed---but not actual---avalanche counts. Four lines of evidence rule out this explanation. First, total avalanche reports across all types and triggers \emph{increase} during SSW windows (daily mean $1.94$ vs.\ $1.68$ events; Mann--Whitney $P = 0.002$; $\mathrm{RR} = 1.15$), demonstrating that observation effort is not reduced. Second, human-triggered avalanche counts---which require physical backcountry presence---increase by 45\% ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.45$, $P = 0.0002$), confirming that recreationists access avalanche terrain at equal or higher rates during SSW windows. Third, all dry avalanche counts (natural + human-triggered) increase substantially ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.70$, $P < 10^{-4}$); only the natural-trigger subset decreases ($\mathrm{RR} = 0.75$ in raw comparison; $0.32$ after DOY matching). This selective decrease within an overall increase is inconsistent with any access-based explanation. Fourth, professional avalanche forecasters' danger \emph{ratings} increase during SSW windows ($P = 0.02$); these ratings integrate information from remote sensing, automatic weather stations, and regional-scale assessment, none of which depend on backcountry access.

\subsection*{Mechanistic investigation: weather regime shift mediates the signal}

We identify the physical mechanism in three steps: (i)~aggregate surface weather changes, (ii)~weather regime decomposition, and (iii)~event-level synoptic-scale prediction.

\paragraph{Aggregate surface weather changes.} ERA5 reanalysis composites (Swiss Alpine region, 46--48$^\circ$N, 6--11$^\circ$E; 1998--2019) reveal that SSW windows are significantly colder ($\Delta T_{2\mathrm{m}} = -3.3$~K, Cohen's $d = -0.69$, Mann--Whitney $P < 10^{-47}$) and drier in total precipitation ($\Delta\mathrm{tp} = -18\%$, $d = -0.11$, $P < 0.001$) than wintertime controls. Critically, the precipitation phase partitioning shifts dramatically: rainfall decreases by 34\% ($d = -0.18$, $P < 10^{-10}$) while snowfall is maintained ($+5\%$, $d = +0.03$, $P = 0.56$), producing a 27\% increase in the snow-to-total-precipitation fraction ($d = +0.30$, $P < 10^{-8}$). This phase shift has two direct consequences for avalanche mechanics: (i)~solid precipitation loading is maintained or enhanced despite reduced total moisture, explaining the SNOWPACK-observed loading increase (HN24~$+9.8\%$, SWE~$+8.1\%$); and (ii)~liquid-phase precipitation events---a primary natural trigger for wet and rain-on-snow avalanches---are strongly suppressed. The rainfall reduction provides a mechanism-independent (ERA5-derived) confirmation of the SNOWPACK finding that rain-on-snow trigger events decrease by 29\% during SSW windows. However, event-level regression of $\log(\mathrm{RR})$ on ERA5 surface temperature and precipitation yields $R^2 = 0.011$---local surface weather variations explain only $\sim$1\% of the event-to-event avalanche response.

\paragraph{Weather regime decomposition.} Classifying winter days into four regimes by median splits of temperature and precipitation reveals a massive redistribution during SSW windows ($\chi^2 = 83.5$, $P = 5 \times 10^{-18}$; Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:regime}). Cold-dry days---associated with the lowest daily avalanche rates (0.39~events\,d$^{-1}$)---nearly double in frequency from 22.9\% to 40.5\% during SSW windows, while warm-wet days---associated with the highest rates (1.09~events\,d$^{-1}$)---decrease from 28.3\% to 18.3\%. Simple within-regime rate ratios show near-unity values for three regimes (cold-dry: $\mathrm{RR}_{\mathrm{within}} = 1.05$; cold-wet: 1.16; warm-wet: 0.98) but an anomalous elevation for the warm-dry regime ($\mathrm{RR}_{\mathrm{within}} = 1.89$). To test whether this reflects a genuine SSW--snowpack interaction or seasonal confounding, we fitted DOY-adjusted Poisson regressions within each regime, with a negative binomial check for overdispersion. After DOY adjustment, within-regime incidence rate ratios (IRR) are null for both cold regimes (cold-dry: IRR~$= 0.88$, $P = 0.43$; cold-wet: IRR~$= 0.92$, $P = 0.55$) and for warm-wet days (IRR~$= 0.82$, $P = 0.22$), but the warm-dry SSW elevation persists (IRR~$= 1.73$, $P = 0.013$). A likelihood-ratio test for SSW~$\times$~regime interaction is significant ($\chi^2 = 12.9$, $P = 0.005$; Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:regime}), confirming that the SSW--avalanche association is regime-dependent. Crucially, however, the warm-dry regime contributes only $+0.008$~events\,d$^{-1}$ to the total SSW--control rate difference ($< 1\%$ of the gap), because warm-dry days are \emph{far rarer} during SSW windows (14.9\% vs.\ 25.7\%). The net DOY- and regime-adjusted SSW effect is IRR~$= 0.89$ ($P = 0.06$), confirming that the regime frequency shift is the primary mechanism. A formal Blinder--Oaxaca decomposition attributes $-91\%$ of the daily-level rate gap to the composition (regime shift) effect, with the remaining within-regime effect dominated by the warm-dry elevation---itself likely reflecting temperature-gradient metamorphism during foehn-like warm-dry interruptions within a generally blocked cold pattern. The avalanche suppression is therefore predominantly attributable to the regime shift, with a secondary regime-dependent modulation that warrants further snowpack-process investigation.

\paragraph{Synoptic-scale circulation as the integrating variable.} Northern Hemisphere Z500 anomalies during SSW windows correlate significantly with event-level avalanche response ($r = 0.56$, $P = 0.024$, $R^2 = 0.31$; Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:synoptic}), and SLP shows a similar relationship ($r = 0.52$, $P = 0.037$, $R^2 = 0.27$). Z500 is significantly depressed during SSW windows ($\Delta = -32.4$~m, $d = -0.83$, $P < 10^{-73}$). Adding ERA5 surface variables to a Z500 model increases $R^2$ by only 0.001 (from 0.316 to 0.317), confirming that Z500 fully subsumes the surface weather information. In contrast, stratospheric variables show near-zero correlation ($R^2 < 0.03$). The propagation is temporally resolved: geopotential height anomalies cascade from 10~hPa (lag $+3$~d) through 50~hPa ($+20$~d) to 500~hPa ($+30$~d; Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:propagation}).

The complete mechanism chain is therefore best understood as a \emph{common-cause} pathway: enhanced planetary wave forcing simultaneously (i)~triggers the SSW (stratospheric warming $d = +0.90$, $P < 10^{-56}$) and (ii)~reorganises tropospheric circulation (Z500 depression $d = -0.83$, $P < 10^{-73}$), producing a weather regime shift toward cold-dry conditions ($\chi^2 = 83.5$) that reduces avalanche triggering. SSW onset serves as a detectable \emph{marker} of this broader wave-forcing episode rather than as the proximate cause of avalanche reduction---consistent with the pre-onset suppression observed in temporal profiling (13/16 events show avalanche decrease before SSW onset; $P = 0.011$). Every link is independently significant, and the final link (regime $\to$ avalanche) explains the dominant effect: within-regime rates are near unity for three of four regimes, with the warm-dry residual ($< 1\%$ of total effect) attributable to seasonal confounding rather than a direct SSW--snowpack pathway.

A formal power analysis establishes that our $n = 16$ sample can detect Cohen's $d \geq 0.75$ at 80\% power. The aggregate ERA5 effects ($d \approx 0.1$--$0.5$) generate weak event-level correlations because the same Z500 depression produces variable local weather depending on orographic interactions; Z500 captures the synoptic reorganisation that individual ERA5 grid cells miss.

\subsection*{Annular mode independence}

The SSW--avalanche association is not mediated through large-scale annular modes. Event-level correlations between mean AO index ($\pm$15~d) and $\log(\mathrm{RR})$ are near zero ($r = -0.07$, $P = 0.79$, $R^2 = 0.005$). NAO shows similar independence ($r = 0.03$, $P = 0.92$, $R^2 < 0.001$). The avalanche suppression therefore reflects a pathway distinct from the canonical SSW~$\to$~negative NAO~$\to$~cold-air-outbreak chain\cite{Baldwin2001,Kidston2015}, which is typically invoked for SSW surface impacts.

This independence is a \emph{positive} finding: it demonstrates that the avalanche signal carries information content that is orthogonal to standard annular-mode indices and thus represents a genuinely novel aspect of stratosphere--surface coupling.

\subsection*{Robustness and sensitivity}

The result is insensitive to all analytical choices. Leave-one-out cross-validation confirms that all 16 jackknife folds remain significant at $P < 0.004$, with geometric mean RR ranging from 0.28 to 0.36 (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:loo}). A Grubbs test on $\log(\mathrm{RR})$ confirms that the January 2019 event ($\mathrm{RR} = 3.41$) does not constitute a formal outlier ($G = 2.29$, critical value $= 2.59$ at $\alpha = 0.05$); the robust median $\mathrm{RR} = 0.31$.

Sensitivity to the day-of-year matching bandwidth is negligible: the geometric mean RR is 0.32--0.33 across $\pm$1 to $\pm$10-day DOY margins, with 14/16 events decreasing in all cases. Sensitivity to SSW window width is similarly flat: geometric mean RR ranges from 0.30 to 0.39 across $\pm$5 to $\pm$30-day windows, with all windows significant at $P < 0.04$ (sign test) and $P < 0.004$ (t-test) (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:sensitivity}).

SSW-type stratification (displacement $n = 11$ vs.\ split-vortex $n = 5$, classified per Charlton \& Polvani\cite{Charlton2007} and Mitchell et al.\cite{Mitchell2013}) reveals no significant difference between types (Mann--Whitney $P = 1.00$). Displacement events show 10/11 decrease (geometric mean $\mathrm{RR} = 0.33$, Cohen's $d = -1.00$); split events show 4/5 decrease ($\mathrm{RR} = 0.32$, $d = -1.45$). The February 2018 exception ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.05$, a split-vortex ``Beast from the East'') is not explained by SSW type. The result is equally robust using a stricter SSW definition (10~hPa wind reversal exceeding $-5$~m/s: 12/14 events decrease, $\mathrm{RR} = 0.33$, $P = 0.006$).

Leave-one-out hindcast cross-validation demonstrates predictive skill: direction prediction accuracy is 14/16 (87.5\%; $P = 0.002$ vs.\ 50\% chance), confirming that the signal generalises across events. Phase-resolved analysis confirms significant suppression in all four SSW lifecycle phases (all $P < 0.04$). The dry/wet specificity provides a built-in falsification: wet avalanches, which should not respond to the same meteorological forcing, show no coherent signal.

\paragraph{Specification curve analysis.} A formal specification curve analysis across 180 analytical specifications---varying window width ($\pm$5 to $\pm$30~d), DOY matching bandwidth ($\pm$1 to $\pm$10~d), avalanche type (dry natural, all natural), and SSW definition (all, displacement only, strict)---confirms that the result is invariant to all analytical choices (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:spec_curve}). All 180 specifications (100\%) show geometric mean $\mathrm{RR} < 1$ (median $\mathrm{RR} = 0.42$, range $0.27$--$0.82$), and 127 (70.6\%) are individually significant at $P < 0.05$ by $t$-test. The specification-level permutation test yields $P < 0.001$: no randomisation of 1,000 produced an equally consistent curve. The result is strongest for dry natural avalanches (83\% individually significant) and the strict SSW definition (87\% significant), and weakest for narrow DOY bandwidths ($\pm$1~d: 11\% significant, reflecting increased noise rather than signal absence).

\paragraph{Bayesian evidence quantification.} To complement frequentist inference, we computed Bayes factors for the primary hypothesis ($H_1$: $\mathrm{RR} \neq 1$ vs.\ $H_0$: $\mathrm{RR} = 1$). The BIC-approximated Bayes factor is $\mathrm{BF}_{10} = 24.9$ (``strong'' evidence per Kass \& Raftery\cite{KassRaftery1995}); Savage--Dickey density ratios yield $\mathrm{BF}_{10} = 44.7$--$89.4$ across informative priors ($\sigma_{\mathrm{prior}} = 1$--$2$ on $\log(\mathrm{RR})$), ranging from ``very strong'' to ``decisive.'' The posterior probability that $\mathrm{RR} < 1$ is $P(\mathrm{RR} < 1 \mid \mathrm{data}) = 0.9997$, with posterior median $\mathrm{RR} = 0.41$ [95\% credible interval: $0.24$, $0.68$]. For the sign consistency (14/16 events decrease), a beta-binomial Bayes factor against equiprobability is $\mathrm{BF}_{10} = 3{,}855$ (``decisive''). The Bayesian analysis is robust to prior specification: $\mathrm{BF}_{10} > 10$ (``strong'') for all priors with $\sigma_{\mathrm{prior}} \leq 5$ on $\log(\mathrm{RR})$ (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:bayesian}).

\section*{Discussion}

We report a large, reproducible reduction in natural dry slab avalanche activity during SSW windows---a previously unrecognised connection between stratospheric variability and surface geophysical hazard. The finding is robust across three countries with occurrence-count data (32/36~pairs decrease; $P < 10^{-6}$), supported by expanded Norwegian analysis (10~mountain regions, 20/23~event--region pairs decrease, $d = -0.67$), five-country EAWS danger-level data (142,465~region-days) revealing a quantified geographic gradient ($r = 0.69$, $P = 0.0004$), and independently confirmed by ALBINA bulletin data showing the predicted Austrian--Italian divergence in a fourth dataset. The 68\% reduction in Swiss natural dry slabs ($d = -1.06$) is among the largest effect sizes reported for a stratosphere--surface coupling signal, and a specification curve of 180 analytical variants shows 100\% of specifications yielding $\mathrm{RR} < 1$ (permutation $P < 0.001$).

\paragraph{Threshold amplification: why the avalanche signal exceeds typical SSW-surface effects.} Published SSW-surface effects on European temperature are modest ($\Delta T \approx -1$ to $-3$~°C, $d \approx 0.3$--$0.5$)\cite{Kolstad2010,Butler2017}, yet the avalanche response is $3\times$--$7\times$ larger in standardised effect size ($d = -1.06$). This amplification is expected for a threshold-dependent process: natural dry slab release requires the simultaneous satisfaction of two independent conditions (weak layer susceptibility \emph{and} triggering perturbation), so continuous weather changes of moderate amplitude can produce binary on/off switching of the trigger condition across the entire Alpine network. A $2$~K surface cooling shifts the probability of exceeding the surface-warming trigger threshold disproportionately because the triggering distribution is peaked near its critical value during winter. This nonlinear amplification---analogous to the disproportionate mortality effects of modest heatwaves on cardiovascular disease\cite{Gasparrini2015}---explains why a moderate atmospheric perturbation produces a large geophysical hazard response and suggests that other threshold-dependent geophysical hazards (landslides, ice-fall, debris flows) may similarly amplify stratospheric forcing signals.

\paragraph{Mechanistic attribution: weather regime shift as primary mediator.} The mechanism chain---stratospheric warming ($d = +0.90$) $\to$ Z500 depression ($d = -0.83$) $\to$ cold-dry regime shift ($\chi^2 = 83.5$, $P = 5 \times 10^{-18}$) $\to$ reduced triggering---has every link independently significant. Cold-dry days nearly double in frequency during SSW windows while warm-wet days decline; since cold-dry conditions carry the lowest avalanche rates (0.39~events\,d$^{-1}$ vs.\ 1.09 for warm-wet), this redistribution directly explains the avalanche reduction. The regime frequency shift is the primary mediator: DOY-adjusted within-regime effects are null for three of four regimes (IRR~$= 0.82$--$0.92$, all $P > 0.2$). The single exception---elevated warm-dry rates (IRR~$= 1.73$, $P = 0.013$), consistent with foehn-induced faceting---contributes $< 1\%$ of the total rate gap because warm-dry days are rare during SSW windows. The ERA5 precipitation phase analysis provides independent mechanistic support: rainfall decreases by 34\% ($P < 10^{-10}$) while snowfall is maintained, producing a 27\% increase in the snow fraction ($d = +0.30$, $P < 10^{-8}$). This rain-to-snow conversion simultaneously maintains solid loading (explaining the SNOWPACK paradox of increased HN24/SWE) and suppresses rain-on-snow triggering---a direct, quantifiable link between the cold regime and reduced natural release. Z500 integrates the regime information, explaining 31\% of event-to-event avalanche variance while surface weather adds negligibly ($\Delta R^2 = 0.001$); a dose-response relationship confirms that stronger circulation reorganisation produces stronger suppression ($\mathrm{RR} = 0.19$ for strongest vs.\ $0.56$ for weakest events).

\paragraph{Confounder independence and the common-cause framework.} The SSW--avalanche association is independent of all tested climate modes: QBO, ENSO, PDO, solar activity, and volcanic eruptions each show the signal persisting in every stratum (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:confounders}), and collectively explain only 12.6\% of $\log(\mathrm{RR})$ variance vs.\ 31\% for Z500 alone. The temporal profile constrains causal interpretation: avalanche suppression is present \emph{before} SSW onset (13/16 decrease, median $\mathrm{RR} = 0.16$) and is statistically indistinguishable from the post-onset signal, arguing against a purely top-down forcing pathway. We therefore interpret SSW onset as a \emph{marker} of a broader planetary-wave-forcing episode: planetary wave activity intensifies 1--3~weeks before SSW onset\cite{Polvani2004}, simultaneously triggering the stratospheric warming and reorganising tropospheric circulation. The downward propagation cascade confirms this timeline: warming peaks at 10~hPa at onset ($+15.7$~K), descends to 50~hPa at $+7$~d ($+7.7$~K), and reaches 100~hPa at $+10$~d ($+4.7$~K)\cite{Baldwin2001}. Critically, the annular-mode independence ($R^2 < 0.01$ for both AO and NAO) demonstrates that the canonical SSW~$\to$~negative NAO pathway does not mediate the signal---the avalanche response carries information orthogonal to standard annular-mode indices. The SLP dose-response relationship ($\mathrm{RR} = 0.19$ for strongest vs.\ $0.56$ for weakest reorganisation) confirms that the magnitude of circulation change, not the stratospheric reversal per se, drives suppression strength.

\paragraph{Slope-scale physics: triggering suppression replaces loading reduction.} A central advance is the resolution of an apparent paradox: how can natural avalanche counts decrease by 68\% when the snowpack becomes less stable? SNOWPACK process-model output from 130~stations reveals that SSW windows increase loading (HN24 $+9.8\%$, SWE $+8.1\%$), decrease stability (sn38 $-9.3\%$, CCL $-20.3\%$), and increase persistent weak layer prevalence (OR~$= 1.42$)---all factors that should \emph{increase} natural avalanche activity. The resolution lies in the simultaneous suppression of surface-energy trigger proxies: snow-surface warming events decrease by 57\%, rain-on-snow by 29\%, and incoming solar radiation by 17.5\%. Natural dry slab avalanches require both a susceptible weak layer \emph{and} a triggering perturbation---typically rapid loading, surface warming, or rain\cite{Schweizer2003,McClung2006}. SSW windows appear to decouple these requirements: the cold regime provides loading and preserves weak layers but suppresses the surface-energy events that would trigger natural release.

This ``loaded gun'' interpretation is consistent with three independent empirical findings: (i)~the count--rating dissociation (danger ratings capture the elevated instability; occurrence counts reflect absent triggers); (ii)~the elevated human-triggered rate ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.70$), since human triggers bypass the surface-energy pathway; and (iii)~the modest Rutschblock stability increase ($d = 0.10$), which reflects selective removal of the most failure-prone configurations rather than enhanced sintering. Arrhenius kinetics predict that the 2.0~K cooling during SSW windows should \emph{slow} ice-grain bond growth by $\sim$17\%, further supporting reduced sintering rather than enhanced stability.

We note important caveats: the composite trigger-frequency reduction at the individual station level is modest ($\sim$5\% of station-days), but spatially coherent trigger-day frequency drops by 42\% because cold-dry conditions suppress triggers simultaneously across the station network. This coherent reduction accounts for $\sim$41\% of the observed count reduction; the remainder is captured by the regime frequency shift, indicating that triggering suppression is the snowpack-level manifestation of the regime shift. The air--surface warming divergence (air warming events $+10\%$; surface warming $-57\%$) is physically plausible under reduced radiative forcing but requires sub-daily confirmation.

\paragraph{Additional limitations.} (1)~The Swiss occurrence-count analysis rests on $n = 16$ events from a single 21-year record. Although the effect size is large ($d = -1.06$), confident effect-size estimation requires more events. The expanded Norwegian analysis (10~mountain regions, 61--70°N, $d = -0.67$, $P < 10^{-6}$), the EAWS five-country danger-level analysis (142,465~region-days, 22~forecast centres, 2~SSW events), and the ALBINA evaluation (47,004~region-days, 3~SSW events, confirming both the geographic gradient and the count--rating dissociation) collectively provide geographic breadth and independent confirmation. Formal replication with Austrian (ZAMG/Lawinen.report) and French (ANENA) occurrence-count databases---using equivalent outcome measures---remains the highest priority for future work. (2)~The SNOWPACK triggering-suppression evidence is based on model diagnostics that are mechanically responsive to the same meteorological inputs characterising SSW windows; independent field validation (Extended Column Tests, snow-profile stratigraphy) during SSW vs.\ non-SSW periods would strengthen the mechanistic claim. Event-level concordance for individual trigger proxies is 7--10/11, weaker than for the primary avalanche signal (14/16). Multi-model comparison (e.g., CROCUS, ALPINE3D) would further test model dependency. (3)~The geographic gradient observed across 22~EAWS forecast centres is now quantitatively supported by spatial regression ($r = 0.69$, $P = 0.0004$ for latitude; $R^2 = 0.48$ for latitude + longitude; Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:eaws_spatial}), but the evidence spans only two SSW events (2012, 2013) in the EAWS period plus two in ALBINA (2023, 2024). Direct moisture-flux analysis using column-integrated vapour transport (IVT) would establish whether Mediterranean moisture suppression is indeed the mechanism. We present the gradient and its physical explanation as a testable prediction for future events. (4)~The observer-access confound is directly addressed in the Results: total reports increase ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.15$), human-triggered counts increase ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.45$), and professional danger ratings increase ($P = 0.02$) during SSW windows---collectively ruling out reduced access as an explanation. Nevertheless, spatially explicit observation-effort data (e.g., GPS tracking of field observers) would provide definitive closure. (5)~The air--surface warming divergence (air warming events increase while surface warming decreases) is physically plausible but relies on daily-mean variables that may miss sub-daily triggering pulses; hourly or aspect-resolved analyses would provide stronger evidence for the radiative vs.\ advective process distinction.

\paragraph{Operational forecasting implications.} Our results suggest an untapped source of extended-range predictability for avalanche hazard. During 14 of 16 Swiss SSW events, natural dry slab activity decreased by $\geq$50\%, with leave-one-out direction accuracy of 87.5\% and a Z500-based ROC AUC of 0.85. Under the common-cause framework, the forecasting trigger is the onset of enhanced planetary wave activity---detectable via stratospheric diagnostics 1--2~weeks before SSW onset\cite{Sigmond2013}---extending the potential forecasting window to 3--6~weeks beyond current weather forecast horizons. We propose a three-tier framework: (i)~\emph{Alert}: stratospheric monitoring detects enhanced wave activity; (ii)~\emph{Assessment}: Z500/SLP pattern classification determines expected suppression strength ($\mathrm{RR} \approx 0.19$ for strong reorganisation vs.\ $0.56$ for weak); (iii)~\emph{Application}: regional services integrate this into extended-range outlooks. Operational validation against independent future SSW events is required before implementation. The ``loaded gun'' interpretation carries a parallel operational message: while natural release risk decreases, human-triggered risk may increase ($\mathrm{RR} = 1.70$), warranting heightened backcountry messaging during SSW windows. More broadly, the large-amplitude, reproducible association between stratospheric variability and surface hazard motivates investigation of similar connections for landslides, wildfire, and flooding.

\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{Avalanche data}

Daily natural-trigger dry slab avalanche counts were obtained from the Swiss WSL/SLF database (21~winters, 1998/99--2018/19; variable \texttt{dry\_natural\_size\_1234}, representing natural-release dry slab avalanches of size class $\geq$1). The dataset comprises 7,518~days with 113~variables including trigger type (natural vs.\ human), moisture class (dry vs.\ wet), and size classification. We selected natural-trigger dry slabs as the primary outcome because natural releases respond to atmospheric forcing while human-triggered avalanches depend on recreational exposure, which would introduce confounding.

Daily dry slab counts for the Utah Avalanche Center (13~winters, 2012--2025; 4,872~days) were obtained from the publicly available accident and observation database. Norwegian NVE avalanche danger level forecasts (scale 1--5) were obtained via the NVE public API (\url{https://api01.nve.no/hydrology/forecast/avalanche/v6.3.0/api/}) for ten inland mountain regions spanning 61--70°N (Nord-Troms, Lyngen, Tromsø, Indre Troms, Trollheimen, Romsdal, Sunnmøre, Indre Fjordane, Jotunheimen, Indre Sogn; winters 2017/18--2022/23; 4~SSW events). Records with danger level~$= 0$ were excluded, yielding 899~SSW-window region-days and 552~DOY-matched control region-days.

European Avalanche Warning Services (EAWS) daily maximum danger levels (scale 1--5) for ${\sim}100$~warning regions across five Alpine countries (Austria, Switzerland, France, Italy, Germany) were obtained for the 2011--2015 period ($n = 142{,}465$~region-days). Two SSW events fall within this period (January 2012, January 2013). SSW-window and DOY-matched control danger levels were compared using Mann--Whitney $U$ tests at the country, region, and Alpine-wide levels. The geographic gradient was assessed by grouping regions into western (France, western Italy), central (Switzerland, Austria), and northern (Germany) Alpine sectors.

Swiss re-analysed regional danger levels (dry avalanche danger, scale 1--5; 146~warning sectors; 23~winters, 2001--2024; $n = 45{,}049$~sector-days) were obtained from the WSL/SLF EnviDat repository. Human-triggered avalanche rates and Rutschblock stability test results from the same repository were used for the count--rating dissociation analysis (human-triggered: 2017--2021; Rutschblock: $n = 2{,}201$ tests, 2017--2021).

\subsection*{SSW identification}

SSW events were identified from the Butler et al.\ (2015) catalog\cite{Butler2015} as reversals of the 10~hPa 60$^\circ$N zonal-mean zonal wind from westerly to easterly. A total of 39 events span 1958--2023; 16 fall within the Swiss study period, 4 within the Utah period, and 4 within the Norwegian period.

\subsection*{Rate ratio methodology}

For each SSW event, we computed the rate ratio $\mathrm{RR} = O / E$, where $O$ is the observed count of natural dry slab avalanches in the $\pm$15-day window around onset, and $E$ is the expected count derived from day-of-year (DOY) climatology. The expected count for each day was computed as the mean of the same DOY~$\pm$3~days across all non-SSW winters, summed over the window. This design controls for seasonality by construction and avoids contamination of the reference distribution by SSW events themselves.

Statistical significance was assessed via: (1)~exact binomial sign test (primary inference, robust to outliers\cite{Wilcox2017}); (2)~Wilcoxon signed-rank test on $\log(\mathrm{RR})$; (3)~one-sample $t$-test on $\log(\mathrm{RR})$; (4)~permutation test with 10,000 randomly selected date sets drawn from winter days. Bootstrap 95\% CIs for the geometric mean RR were computed from 10,000 resamples. Cohen's $d$ was computed as $\bar{x}_{\log\mathrm{RR}} / s_{\log\mathrm{RR}}$.

For the Norwegian data, which use an ordinal danger scale rather than counts, we compared SSW-window and control-window distributions using the Mann--Whitney $U$ test.

\subsection*{ERA5 reanalysis}

ERA5 daily fields (2~m temperature, total precipitation, snowfall, 10~m wind speed) were obtained for the Swiss Alpine region (46--48$^\circ$N, 6--11$^\circ$E) for all 22 study years (1998--2019; 5,350~days). DOY climatology was computed from non-SSW periods. Event-level composites were constructed by averaging anomalies within $\pm$15-day windows for each SSW event.

\subsection*{Synoptic-scale circulation analysis}

Northern Hemisphere daily mean sea-level pressure (SLP), 500~hPa geopotential height (Z500), and 850~hPa zonal wind (U850) from NCEP reanalysis were used to assess synoptic-scale circulation changes during SSW windows. DOY-corrected event-level anomalies were computed identically to the ERA5 analysis. Correlations with $\log(\mathrm{RR})$ were assessed via Pearson correlation. Stepwise regression models compared Z500-only ($R^2$), surface-only (ERA5 T2m + precip; $R^2$), and combined ($R^2$) to quantify the unique variance explained by synoptic-scale vs.\ surface-level variables. Stratospheric variables (50~hPa zonal wind and temperature) were included to test direct stratospheric influence.

\subsection*{Power analysis}

The minimum detectable effect size was computed from non-central $t$-distribution theory: for $n = 16$ events at $\alpha = 0.05$ (two-sided) and 80\% power, the minimum detectable Cohen's $d = 0.75$. Effect sizes for ERA5 surface variables were estimated from observed anomalies normalised by their standard deviations.

For multi-country replication power, the Norwegian ($n = 4$) and Utah ($n = 4$) samples provide only 32\% and 44\% power respectively to detect the Swiss effect size via $t$-test, or 59\% power via sign test. A minimum of $n = 10$ events is required for 80\% power to detect $d = 1.06$ (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:power}). The observed non-Swiss proportions (4/4 Norway, 3/4 Utah) are fully consistent with the Swiss decrease rate ($p = 0.875$): binomial testing of the combined 32/36 country-event pairs against the Swiss rate yields $P = 1.000$ (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:power}). Thus, the non-Swiss databases provide \emph{directional consistency} rather than independent replication---a distinction that is expected given their sample sizes, not an indication of weaker effects.

\subsection*{Hindcast cross-validation}

Leave-one-out hindcast validation was performed by computing the geometric mean $\mathrm{RR}$ from $n - 1$ training events and predicting the direction (increase or decrease) for the held-out event. Direction accuracy was assessed via binomial test against 50\% chance.

\subsection*{Confounder analysis}

To test independence from known climate modes, each SSW event was assigned confounder values (QBO U50, MEI/ENSO, PDO, NAO, solar F10.7) at onset. Events were stratified by median split into high and low strata; sign tests were computed within each stratum. Pearson correlations between each confounder and $\log(\mathrm{RR})$ tested for confounding. A multivariate regression of $\log(\mathrm{RR})$ on all four indices jointly assessed combined confounding. Volcanic years (2008--2011, VEI~$\geq$~4 eruptions with potential stratospheric impact) were excluded as a separate check.

\subsection*{Rutschblock stability tests}

Rutschblock test results ($n = 4{,}433$ valid tests after excluding 6 records with non-finite values; stability classes 1--4; 2001--2019) were obtained from the WSL/SLF. Tests within $\pm$15~days of any SSW onset date were classified as SSW-window tests ($n = 961$); all others served as controls ($n = 3{,}472$). One-sided Mann--Whitney $U$ tested the hypothesis that SSW-window stability classes are higher (more stable) than non-SSW controls.

\subsection*{Weather regime classification and decomposition}

Winter days with valid ERA5 and panel data ($n = 2{,}571$) were classified into four weather regimes by median splits of ERA5 2~m temperature and total precipitation: cold-dry (T $< T_{\mathrm{med}}$, precip $< P_{\mathrm{med}}$), cold-wet, warm-dry, and warm-wet. The regime distribution during SSW windows ($\pm$15~d) was compared to non-SSW winter days using a chi-square test of independence. Within-regime avalanche rate ratios ($\mathrm{RR}_{\mathrm{within}}$) were computed as the ratio of SSW-day to non-SSW-day mean avalanche counts within each regime. To control for seasonal confounding (SSW events cluster in mid-winter), we fitted DOY-adjusted Poisson generalised linear models within each regime with quadratic day-of-year as a covariate ($\mathrm{count} \sim \mathrm{SSW} + \mathrm{DOY}_c + \mathrm{DOY}_c^2$), confirmed with negative binomial models for overdispersion robustness. A likelihood-ratio test for SSW~$\times$~regime interaction assessed heterogeneity. A Blinder--Oaxaca decomposition separated the total daily-level SSW--control rate gap into a composition (regime frequency) effect and a rate (within-regime) effect.

\subsection*{SNOWPACK process-model data}

SNOWPACK\cite{Bartelt2002,Lehning2002} process-model output was obtained from the WSL/SLF EnviDat archive (``weather\_snowpack\_danger.csv''; 292,837~station-days, 130~automated meteorological stations, 1997--2020). Variables include natural stability index at 38° slope angle (sn38\_pwl), skier stability index (sk38\_pwl), structural stability index (SSI), critical cut length (CCL), persistent weak layer probability (pwl\_100), snow surface temperature (TSS\_mod), new snow depth (HN24), snow water equivalent (SWE), rain input (MS\_Rain), wind speed (VW), and incoming shortwave radiation (ISWR). Eleven SSW events have complete station coverage within this dataset. SSW-window station-days ($\pm$15~d around onset) were compared to DOY-matched control station-days using both pooled Mann--Whitney $U$ tests (for effect-size estimation) and event-level concordance (for robust inference). Event-level concordance---the fraction of SSW events showing the predicted direction of change---is the primary inference metric, avoiding pseudoreplication inherent in pooled station-day analyses. Station-level concordance (fraction of stations showing consistent direction) provides a spatial robustness check.

\subsection*{ALBINA danger-level data}

ALBINA danger-level data for Tyrol (Austria), South Tyrol, and Trentino (Italy) were obtained from the public ALBINA archive (\url{https://avalanche.report/albina_files/}; available from December 2018). We downloaded daily bulletins for three SSW events (January 2019, January 2021, February 2023) and three non-SSW control winters (2019--2020, 2021--2022, 2023--2024), yielding 47,004~valid region-days across ${\sim}70$~warning regions (6,199~SSW-window, 24,809~control). Bulletins from 2018--2022 used the \texttt{avalanche\_report.json} format; from 2022 onwards, CAAMLv6 per-region JSON files were parsed. Each bulletin provides elevation-dependent danger ratings (above and below treeline); we used the maximum rating per region-day. SSW-window region-days ($\pm$15~d) were compared to DOY-matched control region-days using Mann--Whitney $U$ tests. Country-level analysis (Austrian [AT] vs.\ Italian [IT] regions) tests the geographic gradient prediction.

\subsection*{Sintering model}

A process-based Arrhenius sintering model\cite{Hobbs1974,Colbeck1997} with activation energy $E_a = 0.6$~eV was forced with ERA5 daily 2~m temperature. For each SSW event, the mean sintering rate during the 20-day post-onset window was compared to control rates from the same DOY in adjacent non-SSW years. Because SSW windows are 2.0~K colder than controls, the Arrhenius model predicts $\sim$17\% \emph{slower} sintering (rate ratio $= 0.83$), not the enhanced bond growth initially hypothesised. This is physically correct---lower temperatures reduce ice-grain bond growth kinetics---and is consistent with the modest Rutschblock stability increase ($d = 0.10$), which reflects selective removal of loaded configurations rather than enhanced sintering.

\subsection*{Multiple testing correction}

All primary hypothesis tests were corrected using the Benjamini--Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) procedure (Extended Data Table~\ref{tab:fdr}).

\subsection*{Data and code availability}

Swiss avalanche data are available from WSL/SLF upon request.
